Self Care

Practical daily habits that support your physical health between doctor consultations.

Medication Adherence

Tips to never miss a dose and take medicines correctly

1

Pair Medicines with a Habit

Link your dose to something you already do daily — morning tea, brushing teeth, or a meal. This "habit stacking" is one of the most reliable ways to avoid missed doses.

2

Use a Weekly Pill Organiser

A simple 7-day organiser lets you see at a glance whether you've taken today's dose. Refill it every Sunday so you never run out mid-week.

3

Never Stop Abruptly Without Advice

Even if you feel better, complete the full prescribed course. Stopping antibiotics or BP medicines early can cause resistance or rebound effects. Always consult your doctor first.

4

Keep a Medicine Log

Note the name, dose, timing, and any side effects in a simple note on your phone. Share it with your doctor at every consultation — it prevents dangerous interactions.

Home Vitals Monitoring

How to accurately track BP, blood sugar & weight at home

🩸
Blood Pressure
Sit for 5 min, no caffeine 30 min prior. Rest arm at heart level. Take 2 readings, 1 min apart.
💉
Blood Sugar
Fasting reading: before breakfast. Post-meal: exactly 2 hours after first bite.
⚖️
Body Weight
Weigh at the same time daily — ideally morning, after using the toilet, before eating.
🌡️
Temperature
Normal range is 36.1–37.2°C. Readings above 38°C indicate fever. Log the time alongside.
★

Share Your Readings With Your Doctor

A week's worth of home readings is far more valuable to your doctor than a single clinic measurement. Take a screenshot of your log and share it before the teleconsult.

Exercise & Movement

Evidence-based activity guidelines for adults

WHO Weekly Target 150 min

150 min of moderate activity per week — about 22 min a day

1

Start with 10-Minute Walks

Three 10-minute walks spread across the day are as effective as a single 30-minute session for cardiovascular health. Start small and build consistency over intensity.

2

Strength Training Twice a Week

Bodyweight exercises like squats, push-ups and planks preserve muscle mass, improve bone density, and support joint health — especially important if you have been sedentary.

3

Reduce Prolonged Sitting

Set a timer to stand and move for 2 minutes every hour. Prolonged sitting independently raises the risk of heart disease even in people who exercise regularly.

4

Listen to Your Body

Sharp joint pain, chest tightness, or dizziness during exercise are red flags. Stop immediately and consult your doctor before resuming activity.

Nutrition & Hydration

Dietary habits that support recovery and long-term health

1

Eat Protein at Every Meal

Protein supports tissue repair and immune function. Aim for a palm-sized portion of lean meat, dal, paneer, eggs, or legumes at every meal — especially during illness or recovery.

2

Reduce Salt for Blood Pressure

The WHO recommends under 5g of salt daily. Avoid packaged snacks, pickles, and papad. Use lemon juice and spices to flavour food instead of extra salt.

3

Drink Water Before Meals

A glass of water 20–30 minutes before eating aids digestion, helps with portion control, and keeps urine pale yellow — the simplest hydration target to hit.

4

Limit Refined Carbs & Sugar

White rice, maida, and sugary drinks spike blood glucose and contribute to insulin resistance over time. Swap to millets, whole wheat, and oats where possible.

Preventive Health Checkups

Screening tests to catch problems before symptoms appear

🩺
Annual GP Visit
Full blood count, lipid panel, kidney and liver function. Every year from age 30.
🫀
Heart Screen
ECG + cholesterol every 2 years from age 35, or earlier if family history exists.
🩸
Diabetes (HbA1c)
Every 3 years from age 35. Every year if overweight or family history of diabetes.
👁️
Eye & Dental
Eye test every 2 years. Dental cleaning every 6 months. Often delayed, always impactful.
★

Don't Wait for Symptoms

Most lifestyle diseases — hypertension, diabetes, high cholesterol — are silent for years. Routine screening is the only way to catch them early, when they are cheapest and easiest to treat.
